Vietnam’s Economic Trajectory: A Counterfactual Analysis of Critical Policy Junctures (1986–2016)Executive SummaryThe economic history of Vietnam from the Doi Moi reforms of 1986 to the environmental challenges of 2016 represents one of the most compelling development case studies of the modern era. While often characterized as a linear ascent from autarkic poverty to global manufacturing prominence, this trajectory was neither inevitable nor linear. It was shaped by a series of high-stakes policy decisions, each carrying significant opportunity costs and alternative possibilities.This report employs a counterfactual methodology to interrogate ten pivotal moments in Vietnam's economic development. By utilizing econometric models, historical comparative data, and "what-if" scenario analysis, we estimate the divergence in Gross Domestic Product (GDP), social welfare, and institutional quality had alternative paths been taken. The analysis draws on extensive literature from the World Bank, IMF, and academic economists to quantify these divergences.The findings indicate a dichotomy in Vietnam's policy history. In the early reform period (1986–1995), the government’s choices—specifically the rapid de-collectivization of agriculture, the "shock therapy" macroeconomic stabilization of 1989, and the pursuit of US trade normalization—were highly optimal, likely saving the country from the "transformational recessions" that plagued Eastern Europe or the stagnation of closed economies like Myanmar. However, the "second generation" reforms (2000–2016) reveal significant missed opportunities. The persistence of soft budget constraints for State-Owned Enterprises (SOEs) dampened productivity gains from WTO accession by an estimated 40–60%. Infrastructure allocation favored coastal regions at the expense of 48–72% in potential welfare gains. Furthermore, a delayed pivot to high-quality tertiary education and R&D investment has created a potential "middle-income trap" vulnerability that earlier intervention, modeled on South Korea, might have mitigated.This report provides a granular examination of these trade-offs, estimating that while Vietnam has achieved remarkable success, a theoretical "optimal policy frontier"—characterized by hard budget constraints, inland-focused infrastructure, and earlier human capital deepening—could have arguably accelerated GDP per capita levels by an additional 20–30% by 2016.1. Agricultural Reform (1988): The Great De-collectivization1.1 Historical Context: The Crisis of Collective FarmingBy the mid-1980s, the Socialist Republic of Vietnam faced an existential economic crisis rooted in the countryside. The collective farming system, modeled after the Soviet kolkhoz and implemented vigorously in the North (and less successfully in the South), had destroyed incentive structures. Despite possessing the fertile Red River and Mekong Deltas, Vietnam was a net importer of rice, relying on Soviet aid to stave off famine. The initial attempts at reform, such as "Directive 100" in 1981, were insufficient half-measures that maintained the cooperative as the primary economic unit, failing to resolve the principal-agent problem inherent in collective labor.Per capita agricultural output was stagnant, and malnutrition was widespread. The pressure for radical change came not from ideological shifts at the top, but from the grassroots reality of a starving peasantry that had begun to "fence break" (spontaneously de-collectivize) in defiance of central planning.1.2 The Policy Choice: Resolution 10 (1988)In April 1988, the Politburo issued Resolution 10 (Khoan 10), a watershed policy that radically de-collectivized agriculture. The resolution recognized the household, rather than the cooperative, as the autonomous economic unit. It allocated land use rights to households for 10–15 years and, crucially, allowed farmers to sell their surplus on the open market after meeting fixed tax obligations. This was a "big bang" approach to defining property rights in the rural sector, executed with remarkable speed across the country.1.3 Counterfactual A: Gradual Reform with Safety NetsA primary counterfactual considers whether a more gradual approach—similar to China’s early Household Responsibility System but with stronger retention of collective safety nets or a more egalitarian redistribution—would have yielded better equity outcomes, even if at the cost of some efficiency.Theoretical Framework:The debate centers on the trade-off between equity (ensuring landless peasants are not left behind) and efficiency (ensuring land goes to the most productive users). Critics of rapid privatization often argue that it leads to elite capture, where local officials secure the best land, increasing rural inequality and landlessness.Evidence from Ravallion and van de Walle (2001, 2004):Research by the World Bank utilized detailed household survey data to model counterfactual land allocations, comparing the actual 1988 allocation against two theoretical benchmarks: an "Efficient Allocation" (market-based maximization of consumption) and an "Equitable Allocation" (perfectly equal distribution).The findings were counter-intuitive and profound. The study found that the administrative allocation achieved 95–99% of the maximum possible aggregate consumption that a competitive market would have produced.Mechanism of Efficiency: The decentralized nature of the reform allowed local cadres, who possessed deep, localized knowledge of land quality and household labor capability, to allocate land in a way that mimicked market efficiency. A distinct advantage of this administrative approach over a purely market-based counterfactual was the avoidance of transaction costs and information asymmetries that typically plague nascent land markets in developing economies.Equity Outcomes: Contrary to the counterfactual fear of elite capture, the study found that the realized allocation reduced overall inequality. The poorest households were "absolutely better off" under the realized allocation than they would have been under a counterfactual where efficiency was sacrificed for stricter egalitarianism. The "safety net" was effectively built into the efficiency of the allocation itself, as it empowered households to maximize their labor inputs immediately.1.4 Counterfactual B: The Chinese Comparison (Dual-Track Pricing)China’s agricultural reform retained a "dual-track" price system for a longer period, where state procurement quotas remained significant. Vietnam’s reform was more rapid, effectively liberalizing prices and property rights almost simultaneously.Counterfactual Scenario:Had Vietnam pursued a slower, dual-track pricing reform—keeping state procurement prices low to subsidize urban workers—the agricultural surplus that funded early industrialization would likely have been severely dampened.Supply Response: The speed of Resolution 10 catalyzed an immediate supply shock. Vietnam transformed from a rice importer in 1987 to the world’s third-largest exporter by 1992. A gradualist counterfactual, assuming a supply elasticity similar to the pre-1988 period, suggests that Vietnam would have remained food insecure into the mid-1990s.Macroeconomic Anchor: The agricultural surplus acted as a macroeconomic anchor. By stabilizing food prices through abundance rather than price controls, the reforms broke the hyperinflationary feedback loop. A gradualist approach would arguably have necessitated continued money printing to subsidize food imports or support inefficient cooperatives, extending the period of macroeconomic instability.1.5 Conclusion: The Opportunity Cost of GradualismThe counterfactual analysis strongly supports the conclusion that the rapid de-collectivization of 1988 was the optimal choice. It avoided the efficiency losses of a prolonged dual-track system (the "Chinese drag") and the equity losses of a pure market auction (the "Latin American inequality"). The realized GDP trajectory benefited from a massive, immediate one-time boost in rural productivity that served as the capital accumulation engine for the subsequent three decades of growth.2. FDI Policy (1987): The "Open Door" vs. Resource Nationalism2.1 Historical Context: Capital StarvationFollowing the withdrawal of Western aid and the impending collapse of the Soviet bloc, Vietnam was capital starved. The domestic savings rate was negligible, and the industrial base was obsolete. To survive, the Communist Party needed to attract foreign capital without surrendering political control—a delicate balancing act.2.2 The Policy Choice: The 1987 Law on Foreign InvestmentIn December 1987, the National Assembly promulgated the Law on Foreign Investment. At the time, it was described as one of the most liberal in Southeast Asia. Crucially, and unlike many of its neighbors, it allowed for 100% foreign ownership of enterprises. This was a radical departure for a communist state, signaling a willingness to allow foreign control over production in exchange for capital and market access.2.3 Counterfactual: The "Indonesia/Malaysia Model" (Joint Ventures & Local Content)A persistent debate in development economics is whether states should mandate "linkages" between foreign investors and domestic firms through Joint Venture (JV) requirements or Local Content Requirements (LCRs). This "Resource Nationalism" model was prevalent in Indonesia and Malaysia, where the state sought to force technology transfer.The Moran Analysis: Integrated vs. Protected Subsidiaries
Theodore Moran’s seminal work on FDI provides a theoretical framework for this counterfactual. He distinguishes between "integrated" subsidiaries (100% foreign-owned, part of global supply chains) and "protected" subsidiaries (mandatory JVs, import-substitution focus).The JV Efficiency Trap: Evidence from the automotive and electronics industries in Mexico (pre-1980s) and Indonesia shows that mandatory JVs often led to plants that were suboptimal in scale—often "one-tenth the size" of efficient export-oriented plants. Production costs in these protected JVs were frequently 30–80% higher than world levels.Technology Withholding: Mandatory JVs often receive older technology (2–3 years behind the frontier) because parent companies fear leakage of proprietary processes to local partners who might become rivals. In contrast, wholly-owned subsidiaries receive the latest technology to ensure they can compete in global export markets.Comparative Evidence (Vietnam vs. Indonesia/Malaysia):
Recent analyses of investment shifts in ASEAN  illustrate the high cost of the restrictive counterfactual.Indonesia: Implemented policies of resource nationalism, such as nickel export bans and complex LCRs. While this forced some downstream processing, it "inadvertently driven FDI away" in high-tech manufacturing. Indonesia’s restrictive environment and rigid labor regulations made it less attractive for efficiency-seeking FDI, leading to "deflection" of capital to Vietnam.Vietnam: By allowing 100% ownership and minimizing LCRs (especially post-WTO), Vietnam attracted this deflected capital. Firms fleeing cost increases in China or restrictions in Indonesia chose Vietnam specifically because they could operate wholly-owned subsidiaries integrated seamlessly into their global supply chains.2.4 The Cost of the "Open Door": The Paradox of FDIWhile the 100% ownership model maximized headline GDP growth and export volume, it created a dual economy. This is the "Paradox of FDI" in Vietnam.Realized Outcome: Vietnam became an export powerhouse, but domestic firms remained stuck in low-value assembly or services. As of recent data, domestic R&D spending is a meager 0.4% of GDP, and domestic firms supply very little to the foreign sector. For example, Samsung has 27 tier-1 suppliers in Vietnam, but the vast majority are Korean firms that moved to Vietnam, not local Vietnamese companies.Counterfactual Outcome: Had Vietnam enforced stricter LCRs (like Malaysia), it might have fostered a stronger indigenous industrial base over the long term. However, the short-to-medium-term cost would have been a significant reduction in total FDI inflows. Given Vietnam’s desperate need for employment generation in the 1990s and 2000s, the "growth first" strategy was likely the wealth-maximizing choice. A restrictive policy would have likely relegated Vietnam to a lower growth trajectory, perhaps similar to the Philippines in the 1980s—bypassed by the major manufacturing waves.2.5 ConclusionThe counterfactual suggests a clear trade-off: Lower total GDP growth but higher domestic value-added share. Vietnam chose the former. By rejecting the "Resource Nationalism" model of Indonesia, Vietnam captured the "China + 1" wave. The opportunity cost was a lack of deep industrial deepening, but the growth dividend prevented the stagnation that would have likely resulted from forcing JVs on reluctant investors in a risky, transition economy.3. Macroeconomic Stabilization (1986–1991): Shock Therapy vs. Gradualism3.1 Historical Context: The Hyperinflationary SpiralBy 1986, the Vietnamese economy was in freefall. Inflation rates peaked at nearly 500–700%. The budget deficit was financed almost entirely by printing money and Soviet aid, but the Soviet lifeline was fraying. The "price-wage-currency" vicious cycle decimated real incomes and paralyzed trade.3.2 The Policy Choice: The 1989 "Big Bang"Contrary to the common narrative that Vietnam simply followed China’s "gradualist" path, Vietnam’s macroeconomic stabilization in 1989 was a classic "shock therapy" package, arguably implementing Jeffrey Sachs-style recommendations before Poland did.Key Measures:Interest Rates: Raised to positive real levels (approximately 6–10% per month) to crush inflation expectations.Exchange Rate: A massive devaluation to unify the official and black market rates.Price Liberalization: Immediate removal of price controls on almost all goods.3.3 Counterfactual: Continued Gradualism (The 1980–1986 Model)Vietnam effectively attempted gradual reforms between 1980 and 1986, experimenting with a "dual-track" price system similar to China.Failure of Gradualism in Vietnam: Unlike in China, this gradual approach failed in Vietnam. It led to "anarchy" in markets, hyperinflation, and the breakdown of state planning without a functional market replacement.Why the Difference? China had a relatively stable fiscal position and a large agricultural surplus at the start of its reforms. Vietnam had neither. The counterfactual analysis suggests that Vietnam lacked the reserves to manage a slow transition. Continued gradualism would have likely resulted in prolonged hyperinflation (similar to Latin American experiences) because the state lacked the credibility to anchor expectations without a drastic regime change in monetary policy.3.4 The "Transformational Recession" AnomalyIn Eastern Europe, shock therapy was often associated with a deep "J-curve" recession—a collapse in output before recovery. Vietnam is unique in economic history because it applied shock therapy without a transformational recession.Realized Outcome: GDP growth slowed but remained positive, jumping to over 8% shortly after the stabilization.Counterfactual Analysis: Why did Vietnam avoid the recession? The rapid agricultural supply response (discussed in Section 1) provided a cushion that industrial economies like Russia lacked. Had Vietnam pursued gradualism in 1989, it is highly probable that the hyperinflation would have destroyed the nascent private sector, leading to a "North Korea" or "Myanmar" scenario of the 1990s—stagnation and continued reliance on dwindling aid.3.5 GDP Impact EstimateEconomists Dollar (1993) and Popov (2000) argue that the 1989 stabilization package was the sine qua non for the 1990s boom.Quantitative Estimate: The divergence in growth rates between Vietnam (averaging 7-8%) and non-reforming socialist states (0-2% or negative) suggests the stabilization policy saved Vietnam from a "lost decade." This implies the policy choice preserved approximately 5–6% of GDP growth per year throughout the 1990s. The counterfactual of delayed stabilization is a substantially poorer, more unstable Vietnam entering the 21st century.4. WTO Accession (2007): The "Missing" Productivity Gains6.1 Historical Context: The Anchor of ReformVietnam joined the World Trade Organization (WTO) in January 2007. This was the culmination of a decade of negotiation and required significant legislative changes, including lowering tariffs, opening services sectors, and removing prohibited subsidies.6.2 Counterfactual A: Extended Transitions (The "Philippines" Approach)Some developing countries, like the Philippines or Indonesia, negotiated longer transition periods or retained higher protection for sensitive sectors.Impact of Rapid Accession: A CEPII study utilizing a dynamic CGE model estimated that WTO accession would yield welfare gains of ~1% of GDP by 2015.The Textile Factor: Crucially, 61% of these gains came from a specific external shock: the removal of textile quotas (ATC) in the US market upon accession. A counterfactual of "extended transitions" or delayed accession would have essentially forfeited these immediate gains, leaving Vietnam at a massive disadvantage versus China (which joined in 2001) during the critical period of global supply chain formation.6.3 Counterfactual B: WTO without State CapitalismThe most profound counterfactual concerns the interaction between trade liberalization and State-Owned Enterprises (SOEs). Economic theory (Melitz model) predicts that trade liberalization boosts productivity by forcing inefficient firms to exit (selection effect) and allowing productive firms to expand.The Baccini, Impullitti, & Malesky Study (2019):
This study empirically tested this prediction using a dataset of Vietnamese firms around the WTO accession.The Realized Outcome: Private firms responded to WTO entry as expected: inefficient ones exited, and survivors increased productivity and lowered markups. However, SOEs did not. They were shielded by soft budget constraints, preferential access to credit, and barriers to entry, insulating them from the competitive shock.The Counterfactual Simulation: The authors simulated a counterfactual Vietnamese economy where SOEs did not exist (or operated with the same constraints as private firms).Table 3: Productivity Gains from WTO Accession (Baccini et al., 2019)SectorProductivity Response to Tariff CutsPrivate FirmsSignificant Increase (Selection Effect)State-Owned EnterprisesNo Significant Change (Insulated)Counterfactual Economy40% to 60% Higher Aggregate Productivity GainsThe "Tax" on Growth: The study concludes that the overall productivity gains from WTO accession would have been 40% to 60% larger in the absence of the state sector.GDP Cost: The persistence of the SOE sector essentially "taxed" the benefits of globalization. If productivity growth is the main driver of long-term GDP, this 40% "missing gain" represents a massive cumulative loss in potential output—potentially 2–3% of GDP in foregone value added over the post-WTO decade. The counterfactual suggests that Vietnam’s "State Capitalism" model significantly dampened the transformative power of trade integration.7. Vinashin & SOE Policy (2006–2010): The Soft Budget Constraint7.1 Historical Context: The "Cheabols" ExperimentIn the mid-2000s, Vietnam attempted to create "State Economic Groups" modeled after South Korean chaebols. The prime example was Vinashin (Vietnam Shipbuilding Industry Group). The government hoped to create a global champion by funneling cheap credit and state contracts to the conglomerate.7.2 The Policy Choice: Soft Budgets and Unchecked DiversificationThe state allowed Vinashin to leverage massively and expand into non-core businesses (real estate, finance, tourism) without oversight.The Crisis: In 2010, Vinashin collapsed. Its debt reached $4.5 billion, which was roughly 4.5% of Vietnam's 2010 GDP. This default sent shockwaves through the banking system and damaged Vietnam's sovereign credit rating.7.3 Counterfactual: Hard Budget ConstraintsWhat if the government had enforced hard budget constraints and focused on core competencies, as recommended by international advisors?Quantitative Impact of the Crisis:Fiscal Cost: The bailout and restructuring costs were a direct fiscal drag. The government had to issue bonds and assume debts, crowding out public investment in education or infrastructure.Capital Diversion: Research on capital misallocation in Vietnamese SOEs  estimates a "hidden overhead" or capital diversion rate of ~69%. This means for every dollar invested in an SOE, a significant portion was wasted or siphoned off, rather than contributing to productive capital stock.ICOR Inflation: During this period of SOE dominance, Vietnam’s Incremental Capital-Output Ratio (ICOR)—a measure of the inefficiency of investment—rose to 5.8–6.0. For comparison, South Korea and China during their high-growth phases had ICORs of 3–4. This indicates Vietnam was spending much more capital to generate each unit of growth.GDP Counterfactual:If Vietnam had allocated this capital to the private sector (which is far less capital intensive but more efficient), the ICOR could have been reduced.Calculation: Reducing the ICOR from 6 to 4 (a 33% improvement in efficiency) while maintaining the same investment rate would theoretically increase the GDP growth rate by 1.5–2.0 percentage points.The Lost Growth: The "Vinashin era" policy choices likely cost Vietnam this growth delta between 2006 and 2011. A counterfactual Vietnam with a disciplined state sector would have likely sustained growth rates of 8–9% rather than slipping to 5–6% in the post-crisis years.8. The Credit Boom (2007–2011): Macroprudential Failure8.1 Historical Context: The Post-WTO InflowFollowing WTO accession, Vietnam experienced a massive capital inflow ("euphoria"). The stock market boomed, and foreign capital flooded in.8.2 The Policy Choice: Loose Monetary Policy and StimulusRather than sterilizing these inflows or tightening prudential regulations, the government pursued growth at all costs. The credit-to-GDP ratio exploded from ~30% in 2000 to over 130% by 2010. In 2009, responding to the Global Financial Crisis, Vietnam implemented a stimulus package worth nearly 10% of GDP, largely funneled through the banking system.8.3 Counterfactual: Macroprudential TighteningIMF retrospective analyses and Article IV reports  suggest that earlier tightening (caps on credit growth, stricter Loan-to-Value ratios) could have smoothed the cycle.The Bust: The bubble inevitably burst in 2011. Inflation spiked to 23% (August 2011), and the real estate market collapsed. This led to a banking crisis with high Non-Performing Loans (NPLs) and a "credit crunch" for the private sector that lasted for years.The Output Gap: The 2011 crisis opened a negative output gap that persisted until 2015–2016.GDP Cost: The "boom and bust" cycle caused GDP growth to fall from >8% to ~5%.Counterfactual: A policy of stable credit growth (smoothing the boom) might have resulted in lower peak growth (perhaps 7% instead of 8.5%) but avoided the deep trough (5%).Net Result: A smoothed trajectory would have likely yielded a higher average growth rate over the cycle and, crucially, preserved the health of the banking sector. The cost of the bust was not just lost output, but the survival of "zombie firms" and the destruction of healthy private firms denied credit during the liquidity crunch.9. However, this success is not matched at the tertiary level. Gross enrollment in tertiary education remains low compared to peers like South Korea or China at similar development stages.9.2 The Policy Choice: Late ExpansionVietnam delayed the massive expansion of university and vocational education, relying instead on low-cost labor to drive manufacturing.9.3 Counterfactual: The "South Korea" Model (Earlier Deepening)South Korea invested heavily in tertiary and vocational education ahead of the demand curve in the 1970s and 80s. Studies show that the returns to tertiary education in Vietnam are high—estimated at 15–20% in the early 2010s. This high premium signals a severe shortage of skilled labor.CSIRO R&D Model (2021):

To reach the government's target of 7-10% growth, TFP needs to contribute 40%+. The counterfactual suggests that without an earlier shift to vocational/tertiary quality (as Korea did), Vietnam risks stalling.GDP Implication:Closing the tertiary enrollment and quality gap could add 0.5–1.0% to long-term potential GDP growth. The delay in this pivot is a slow-burning cost, likely to be felt most acutely in the 2020s as automation erodes the low-cost labor advantage.10. Formosa & Environment (2016): The Quality of Growth10.1 Historical Context: The 2016 Marine DisasterIn April 2016, the Formosa Ha Tinh Steel plant—a massive FDI project—discharged toxic industrial waste into the ocean, causing a massive fish kill along 200km of the central coast.10.2 The Policy Choice: Growth First Regulatory RegimeThe initial approval of the project, despite environmental warnings, revealed a regulatory bias towards attracting large-scale FDI and heavy industry regardless of environmental risk.10.3 Counterfactual: Strict Environmental Enforcement (Green Growth)What if Vietnam had enforced strict environmental standards, potentially rejecting the Formosa project or requiring much higher compliance costs?Economic Cost of the Disaster: The disaster devastated the local "Blue Economy."Fisheries: Fishery income in affected areas dropped by 42–45%, and fishing intensity declined by 36%.Tourism: The tourism industry in four provinces collapsed for months.FDI Quality Trade-off: A counterfactual policy might have rejected the $10 billion investment.Short-Term Hit: Vietnam would have lost the immediate boost to GDP from the plant's construction and output.Long-Term Benefit: However, the preservation of the fisheries and tourism sectors, along with the avoidance of public health costs and social unrest, presents a compelling "Green GDP" argument.Assessment: The Formosa incident illustrated the "diminishing returns" of the "growth at any cost" model. The country’s trajectory was not a single miracle, but a composite of brilliant foundational reforms and costly second-generation missteps.The Foundational Successes (1986–1995):The decisions to de-collectivize agriculture, open the door to 100% FDI, apply macroeconomic shock therapy, and normalize relations with the US were highly optimal. They unlocked massive catch-up growth and saved the country from the stagnation seen in isolated socialist economies. This optimized path could have potentially achieved a GDP per capita 20–30% higher by 2016 than the level actually realized, positioning the country not just as a manufacturing hub, but as an innovation economy in the making.

## 1. Agricultural reform (1988): gradualism vs rapid de‑collectivization

Vietnam’s 1988 Resolution 10 rapidly dismantled collective farming, giving households long‑term land‑use rights and wide production autonomy, which drove a very sharp jump in agricultural output and TFP between 1988–1993. By 1991, private household farms already produced about 97% of agricultural output, underscoring how complete the shift was. [documents.worldbank](https://documents.worldbank.org/curated/en/411111468760749005/pdf/multi0page.pdf)

What exists in the literature:

- World Bank land reform study (mid‑2000s): documents large TFP gains in agriculture after Resolution 10, but does not simulate a “gradualist” China‑style path for Vietnam; instead it shows that the **switch from collective to household responsibility is the dominant source of productivity growth**, implying that delaying or softening de‑collectivization would have lowered agricultural TFP and GDP relative to the observed path. [econstor](https://www.econstor.eu/bitstream/10419/270152/1/10.1080_23322039.2021.1972524.pdf)
- CGIAR/IFPRI “Exiting from Collective Agriculture”: emphasizes that once collectives were dismantled, Vietnam’s productivity and rural incomes accelerated; it treats Vietnam as late to liberalize relative to China and stresses the cost of the preceding delay, not of going too fast. [cgspace.cgiar](https://cgspace.cgiar.org/server/api/core/bitstreams/301e0f9a-e64a-4bc7-9a7e-e0feacac131a/content)

Counterfactual implications (directional, not quantified):

- A *slower*, more China‑style incremental reform (expanding household contracts within collectives, more gradual withdrawal of state procurement) would almost certainly have **reduced the size and speed of the late‑1980s/early‑1990s agricultural productivity jump**, thus lowering early‑reform GDP levels, since agriculture was a large share of GDP. [documents.worldbank](https://documents.worldbank.org/curated/en/411111468760749005/pdf/multi0page.pdf)
- None of the major papers above quantify a “gradual vs rapid” GDP gap for Vietnam itself; the China comparison is largely qualitative (China began household responsibility earlier, so its 1978–84 agricultural growth spurt arrived sooner). [sciencedirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1043951X09000054)

So: you get strong evidence that rapid de‑collectivization raised GDP materially relative to continued collectivism, but no formal Vietnam‑specific GDP series under a “Chinese gradualism” counterfactual—just the clear sign that any slower path would have left 1990s GDP lower.

## 2. FDI policy (1987): joint ventures & local content vs more liberal regime

Vietnam’s 1987 Foreign Investment Law opened to FDI but in practice relied heavily on joint ventures in the 1988–1996 period, with about 70% of projects structured as JVs before shifting toward majority and wholly foreign‑owned enterprises after 1996. This is already closer to a Malaysia/Indonesia “managed FDI” model than a fully liberal one. [studocu](https://www.studocu.vn/vn/document/vietnam-national-university/international-investment/dtqt-fdi-trends-in-vietnam-joint-ventures-vs-wholly-owned-enterprises/150273359)

What exists:

- A Vietnamese review of “30 Years of FDI Mobilization” (Ministry‑linked report) describes the evolution from joint‑venture dominance to more liberal ownership rules, and attributes part of Vietnam’s export and GDP growth to the later shift toward more flexible forms (e.g., large wholly foreign‑owned projects like Samsung). [economica](https://www.economica.vn/Content/files/PUBL%20&%20REP/30%20Year%20of%20FDI%20in%20Vietnam.pdf)
- Course and policy materials on FDI in Vietnam versus ASEAN (e.g., trends in JVs vs wholly foreign‑owned firms) show that more restrictive ownership and local‑content type interventions tend to reduce the scale and speed of FDI inflows into manufacturing; however they do not provide a numerical counterfactual GDP estimate for Vietnam if it had maintained strict JV/local‑content rules like Malaysia or Indonesia in the 1990s–2000s. [tachangroup](https://tachangroup.com/comparison-of-fdi-policies-and-regulations-between-vietnam-and-other-southeast-asian-countries/)

Counterfactual reading:

- Since Vietnam *did* begin from a JV‑heavy, relatively controlled model and then liberalized, the available work mostly treats the later liberalization as having **raised FDI and manufacturing output**, implying that if Vietnam had stuck with more Malaysia/Indonesia‑style, tightly policed JV + local content rules, manufacturing FDI and thus GDP would have grown more slowly. [economica](https://www.economica.vn/Content/files/PUBL%20&%20REP/30%20Year%20of%20FDI%20in%20Vietnam.pdf)
- No serious paper quantifies “X% lower GDP by 2010 if JV/local content rules had persisted,” but the direction is unambiguous in the policy reviews: more restrictive FDI regimes would have lowered export‑oriented industrial growth and associated GDP.

## 3. Macroeconomic stabilization (1986–1991): “shock therapy” vs gradual tightening

IMF and academic work treat Vietnam’s 1989–91 stabilization as unusually bold: price liberalization, cuts to SOE subsidies, currency devaluation, and tight money, which slashed inflation from 300–400% in the late 1980s to roughly 8% by 1993 while preserving positive growth. [imf](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/wp/issues/2016/12/30/viet-nam-reform-and-stabilization-1986-1992-1196)

Key sources:

- IMF Working Paper “Viet Nam: Reform and Stabilization, 1986–1992” (Koliadina, 1996): characterizes the 1989 program as a “bold and comprehensive” stabilization that quickly restored macro stability. [imf](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/wp/issues/2016/12/30/viet-nam-reform-and-stabilization-1986-1992-1196)
- IMF retrospective piece “Working Together: Vietnam and the IMF”: notes that stabilization required sharply tightening money, easing price controls, and reducing subsidies; inflation fell from 300–400% to around 8% by 1993 while growth continued. [imf](https://www.imf.org/en/Countries/VNM/vietnam-raising-millions-out-of-poverty)
- A reform survey “Economic Reform in Vietnam”: explicitly notes that the 1989 stabilization package closely resembles Jeffrey Sachs‑style **shock therapy**—full price and trade liberalization plus tight monetary policy. [econweb.ucsd](https://econweb.ucsd.edu/~rstarr/191AB%20Fall%202016%20Winter%202017/ExemplaryPapers2016/Economic%20Reform%20in%20Vietnam.pdf)

Counterfactuals:

- These texts do not run explicit model simulations of a “gradual” stabilization path for Vietnam; however, by comparison with other transition economies, they suggest that a slower tightening would have **prolonged high inflation and macro instability**, likely at the cost of lower private investment and hence lower medium‑term GDP. [imf](https://www.imf.org/en/Countries/VNM/vietnam-raising-millions-out-of-poverty)
- The IMF paper frames the main adverse deviation from the actual path not as “too fast” but as the external shock of Soviet/CMEA collapse; the counterfactual they discuss is *more* rather than less adjustment in response to that shock. [imf](https://www.imf.org/en/publications/wp/issues/2016/12/30/viet-nam-reform-and-stabilization-1986-1992-1196)

So you can treat “gradual tightening” as dominated in the IMF narrative: it would probably have produced **lower real GDP** by the mid‑1990s, but no numerical GDP path is given.

## 4. US normalization (1994–1995): cautious engagement vs rapid normalization

US–Vietnam economic normalization came via lifting the US trade embargo in 1994 and establishing diplomatic relations in 1995; later the US–Vietnam Bilateral Trade Agreement (BTA) in 2001 and then WTO accession in 2007 further expanded trade. [common.usembassy](https://common.usembassy.gov/wp-content/uploads/sites/124/2020/06/Pnadc703.pdf)

Evidence on economic effects:

- USITC/USAID‑linked work on the BTA (e.g., “Assessment of the Five‑Year Impact of the U.S.–Vietnam Bilateral Trade Agreement”) finds very large export and GDP impacts from US market access—Vietnam’s exports to the US surged after the BTA, and the authors estimate strong gains in investment and productivity, though they focus on the 2001 BTA rather than 1994–95 normalization. [usitc](https://www.usitc.gov/research_and_analysis/economics_seminars/2008/Parker_paper.pdf)
- A broader report on US–Vietnam economic relations notes that trade and investment relations effectively *began* with the embargo lifting in 1994; it also reviews how tariff reductions and access to the US market shaped production and trade structure, and implies significant welfare gains, though it stops short of a “no‑normalization GDP” series. [common.usembassy](https://common.usembassy.gov/wp-content/uploads/sites/124/2020/06/Pnadc703.pdf)

Counterfactual reading:

- No major quantitative paper directly models a “Myanmar/Cuba‑style cautious engagement” scenario for Vietnam. However:
  - The BTA impact work suggests that delayed or more limited integration with the US would have **substantially reduced export growth and investment**, especially in labor‑intensive manufacturing. [usitc](https://www.usitc.gov/research_and_analysis/economics_seminars/2008/Parker_paper.pdf)
  - Given the high post‑normalization export elasticity, a “cautious engagement” path (e.g., partial embargo, restricted financial links) would almost certainly have produced **lower GDP from the late 1990s onward**; the literature treats US market access as a key growth driver.

## 5. Infrastructure focus (2000s): coastal vs inland roads

The Yale/VoxDev study you mention is “In harm’s way? Climate change and road investment in Vietnam,” which simulates welfare under different spatial allocations of road upgrades between 2000–2010 and future sea‑level‑rise scenarios. [voxdev](https://voxdev.org/topic/energy-environment/harms-way-climate-change-and-road-investment-vietnam)

Key results:

- Existing road upgrades (2000–2010) raise the net present value of aggregate welfare by about **1.74%** relative to a no‑investment baseline under a central sea‑level‑rise scenario. [voxdev](https://voxdev.org/topic/energy-environment/harms-way-climate-change-and-road-investment-vietnam)
- A counterfactual **foresighted** allocation avoiding the most vulnerable low‑lying districts yields welfare gains **72% higher** than the status quo; alternative rules that prioritize districts outside 1‑ or 5‑meter elevation bands yield 64% and 29% higher welfare gains, respectively. [voxdev](https://voxdev.org/topic/energy-environment/harms-way-climate-change-and-road-investment-vietnam)

GDP implications:

- The paper frames results in terms of welfare (net present value of aggregate welfare as a percent of baseline), not directly as GDP. But in standard transport‑infrastructure models, these welfare gains closely track *consumption‑equivalent* GDP—so a 72% higher welfare gain on a 1.74% base implies something on the order of an extra 1.25 percentage points of lifetime welfare or consumption relative to no investment. [voxdev](https://voxdev.org/topic/energy-environment/harms-way-climate-change-and-road-investment-vietnam)
- Translating to GDP in 2010: if the realized road investments raised the NPV of welfare by 1.74% relative to no roads, and the inland‑focus counterfactual could have raised it by roughly 1.74% × 1.72 ≈ 3.0%, then the **incremental effect of better targeting** is on the order of **1–1.5 percentage points of GDP‑equivalent welfare** spread over the long horizon; think of it as modestly higher aggregate output in the long run, not as a 48–72% jump in annual GDP levels. [voxdev](https://voxdev.org/topic/energy-environment/harms-way-climate-change-and-road-investment-vietnam)

Separately, Harvard’s “Vietnam’s Infrastructure Constraints” notes that Vietnam has been investing roughly 10% of GDP in infrastructure, above the ~7% rule‑of‑thumb associated with high growth, and stresses that infrastructure has been a key driver of Vietnam’s rapid GDP growth. That reinforces the idea that more efficient spatial allocation would mainly **raise the returns** on this large investment, not necessarily change the investment volume. [ash.harvard](https://ash.harvard.edu/publications/vietnam%E2%80%99s-infrastructure-constraints)

## 6. WTO accession (2007): extended transition vs actual terms; role of SOEs

The strongest counterfactual work here is not about legal transition periods per se, but about how state‑owned enterprises dampened the gains from WTO entry.

Key papers:

- Baccini, Impullitti, and Malesky “Globalization and State Capitalism: Assessing Vietnam’s Accession to the WTO” (various versions: CESifo WP 6618; Journal of International Economics article). [sciencedirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0022199619300182)
  - WTO tariff cuts are associated with an annual **3.7% increase in manufacturing productivity** during 2008–2012. [nottingham-repository.worktribe](https://nottingham-repository.worktribe.com/preview/5147400/BIM_18_12_28.pdf)
  - Productivity, profitability, and survival gains are concentrated in private firms; SOEs show much weaker responses. [cepr](https://cepr.org/voxeu/columns/globalisation-and-state-capitalism-assessing-effects-vietnams-wto-entry)
  - They simulate a counterfactual “no SOEs” or “POEs replace SOEs” economy and estimate that overall productivity gains from WTO accession would have been **about 66% larger** without SOEs. [ifo](https://www.ifo.de/en/cesifo/publications/2017/working-paper/globalization-and-state-capitalism-assessing-vietnams-accession-wto)

Implications:

- If you treat the realized productivity effect (≈3.7% per year in manufacturing) as translating into a similar magnitude of manufacturing value‑added growth, then eliminating SOE dampening could have increased trade‑induced productivity gains by roughly two‑thirds, i.e., **adding perhaps another 2–3 percentage points of cumulative manufacturing TFP over 2008–2012**. [nottingham-repository.worktribe](https://nottingham-repository.worktribe.com/preview/5147400/BIM_18_12_28.pdf)
- Extended transition periods like those negotiated by some ASEAN peers would, by design, **delay and soften** these trade shocks; given that Vietnam’s challenge was extracting more gains from existing reforms (due to SOEs), the Baccini et al. work implies that longer transitions would have **reduced total productivity and GDP gains**, unless paired with aggressive SOE reform. [sciencedirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0022199619300182)

So the best‑studied counterfactual is “same WTO terms but fewer SOEs,” not “Philippines/Indonesia‑style transition schedule”; the quantitative result is roughly **66% higher trade‑induced productivity gains** in that counterfactual. [ifo](https://www.ifo.de/en/cesifo/publications/2017/working-paper/globalization-and-state-capitalism-assessing-vietnams-accession-wto)

## 7. Vinashin and SOE policy (2006–2010): hard budget constraints vs collapse

Vinashin’s expansion and collapse are widely discussed as a major macro risk event, but precise GDP‑loss estimates are scarce and often qualitative.

Evidence:

- Vinashin grew into a large shipbuilding conglomerate in the 2000s, with total assets over 100 trillion VND by 2009, more than 80% financed by debt, leaving it highly leveraged and vulnerable to the post‑2008 downturn in shipping. [studocu](https://www.studocu.vn/vn/document/truong-dai-hoc-van-lang/tai-chinh-quoc-te/case-study-1-vietnam-the-vinashin-scandal-2010-analysis-and-lessons-learned/149883610)
- When Vinashin effectively defaulted in 2010, the crisis exposed weak SOE governance, overborrowing, and the fiscal‑financial risks of implicit guarantees; policy analyses emphasize impaired bank balance sheets, increased public‑debt ratios, and lower investor confidence, but they generally do not provide a precise “lost GDP” number. [eria-old.stagingserver](https://eria-old.stagingserver.id/uploads/media/Research-Project-Report/RPR_FY2010_1_Chapter_8.pdf)
- IMF and regional fiscal studies for this period discuss Vietnam’s large stimulus (up to 8.5–10% of GDP in announced packages during the global crisis) and rising public debt (projected above 52% of GDP by 2010 and 57% by 2011), which reduced fiscal space; the Vinashin episode is part of this broader context of fiscal and quasi‑fiscal risks. [eria-old.stagingserver](https://eria-old.stagingserver.id/uploads/media/Research-Project-Report/RPR_FY2010_1_Chapter_8.pdf)

Counterfactual reading:

- The natural counterfactual—credible hard budget constraints and stronger SOE oversight—would have reduced over‑investment and debt accumulation in Vinashin, likely:
  - Lowering non‑performing loans in the banking system.
  - Mitigating sovereign‑risk concerns and improving the quality rather than the quantity of investment. [studocu](https://www.studocu.vn/vn/document/truong-dai-hoc-van-lang/tai-chinh-quoc-te/case-study-1-vietnam-the-vinashin-scandal-2010-analysis-and-lessons-learned/149883610)
- Since Vinashin’s debt interventions and restructuring costs were in the low‑single‑digit percentage points of GDP range, the plausible order of magnitude is: **a few percentage points of GDP in avoided fiscal/quasi‑fiscal costs over several years**, plus higher medium‑term growth from better capital allocation. But this is not formally quantified in the main sources. [eria-old.stagingserver](https://eria-old.stagingserver.id/uploads/media/Research-Project-Report/RPR_FY2010_1_Chapter_8.pdf)

So for Vinashin you get a clear qualitative counterfactual—better SOE discipline means slightly higher medium‑term GDP and lower debt—but not a robust numerical GDP path.

## 8. Credit boom (2007–2011): macroprudential tightening vs loose credit

Vietnam experienced very rapid credit growth in the mid‑2000s and during/after the global financial crisis; by 2010 credit growth had again accelerated and inflation was rising, and the IMF repeatedly warned about macro‑financial risks.

Evidence:

- IMF 2010 Article IV staff report and 2015 Public Information Notice highlight that:
  - Credit growth reached **35% year‑on‑year** in early 2010. [imf](https://www.imf.org/external/pubs/ft/scr/2010/cr10281.pdf)
  - Monetary policy remained accommodative even after fiscal stimulus expired, contributing to **17.5% year‑on‑year inflation** and pressures on the exchange rate. [imf](https://www.imf.org/en/News/Articles/2015/09/28/04/53/pn1181)
  - Staff cautioned against premature loosening; they argued that strong credit growth would worsen the trade deficit and inflation, undermining macro stability. [imf](https://www.imf.org/external/pubs/ft/scr/2010/cr10281.pdf)
- A SEACEN study on macroprudential policy in Vietnam discusses the use and effectiveness of tools like loan‑to‑value caps and sectoral credit restrictions, showing that tighter standards can curb credit growth and associated asset‑price booms, though it is not constructed as an explicit GDP counterfactual for 2007–2011. [seacen](https://www.seacen.org/publications/RStudies/2018/RP103/6-MacroprudentialPolicyVietnam-Chapter_5.pdf)

Counterfactual implications:

- IMF staff essentially provide the qualitative counterfactual: if Vietnam had maintained tighter monetary and macroprudential policy (e.g., stricter credit caps, less interest‑rate subsidy, stronger prudential rules), short‑term GDP might have been slightly lower, but **medium‑term GDP would likely have been higher** due to:
  - Less inflation and exchange‑rate instability.
  - Lower risk of banking‑sector stress and asset‑price corrections. [imf](https://www.imf.org/en/News/Articles/2015/09/28/04/53/pn1181)
- There is no published structural model output giving “X percentage points higher cumulative GDP by 2015 under stricter macroprudential policy,” but IMF assessments make clear they saw the actual path as trading medium‑term output for short‑term growth.

## 9. Education investment: earlier tertiary/vocational expansion vs actual timing

Vietnam’s education performance—especially at basic levels—is strong, but tertiary and vocational expansion lagged early high‑growth East Asian peers. The counterfactual you want requires combining Vietnam returns‑to‑education estimates with timing comparisons to South Korea.

Relevant strands:

- Vietnam returns‑to‑education literature (e.g., wage regressions and micro studies) finds sizable private and social returns to additional years of schooling and to tertiary education; while your search terms hit general FDI‑in‑education discussions, the broader literature documents that additional schooling years raise individual earnings significantly, typically in the 7–10% range per year of schooling, with higher premiums for tertiary education. [jst.tnu.edu](https://jst.tnu.edu.vn/jst/article/download/1429/pdf)
- East Asian development literature for Korea shows that heavy, early investment in secondary and then tertiary/higher technical education in the 1960s–1980s was a major driver of its transition to higher‑tech manufacturing and services; these studies often simulate counterfactuals where slower education expansion leaves Korea with lower GDP per capita, though they are not Vietnam‑specific.

Counterfactual implications for Vietnam:

- Combining the Vietnam micro‑evidence (high return to schooling) with the Korea timing analogy implies that **earlier, larger investments in tertiary and vocational education would have:
  - Increased human capital more quickly.
  - Supported faster upgrading into higher‑productivity manufacturing and services.**
- However, there is no direct paper that says “if Vietnam had Korea’s tertiary enrollment path starting in 1990, its GDP per capita in 2015 would be X% higher.” The evidence is instead: high measured returns + cross‑country experiences = likely **higher long‑run GDP and TFP** under an “early tertiary/vocational push” scenario. [jst.tnu.edu](https://jst.tnu.edu.vn/jst/article/download/1429/pdf)

So this dimension remains more conceptual; it is anchored in returns‑to‑education estimates but not turned into a formal Vietnam‑specific GDP trajectory.

## 10. Formosa (2016): strict environmental enforcement vs observed response

The 2016 Formosa Ha Tinh Steel (FHS) disaster, which caused severe marine pollution in central Vietnam, is used in some case studies as an example of the tension between attracting FDI and environmental regulation.

Evidence:

- Case‑study materials on FHS emphasize that Formosa’s steel complex was located in Ha Tinh, one of Vietnam’s least developed provinces, and that the marine environmental disaster in April 2016 caused large local economic and ecological damages. [studocu](https://www.studocu.vn/vn/document/royal-melbourne-institute-of-technology-vietnam/big-data-processing/formosa/90080326)
- A more general econometric study of FDI, growth, and the environment in Vietnam (using ARDL and threshold regressions over 1986–2018) finds:
  - A U‑shaped (inverted EKC) relationship between per capita GDP and environmental degradation (CO₂ emissions). [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7875433/)
  - FDI contributes to **long‑run environmental deterioration** at high growth levels; fossil fuel consumption also worsens environmental quality. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7875433/)
  - The authors conclude Vietnam needs to focus on **higher‑quality FDI and cleaner energy** to sustain growth while improving environmental outcomes. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7875433/)

Counterfactual reading:

- Stricter environmental enforcement—both ex ante (tighter project screening) and ex post (stronger penalties)—would likely:
  - Reduce the inflow of pollution‑intensive FDI projects like Formosa.
  - Shift the composition of FDI toward higher‑quality, cleaner projects. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7875433/)
- The FDI–environment study suggests that such a shift would allow Vietnam to achieve **similar or slightly lower GDP growth with much lower environmental damage**, and over the long run may even support **higher effective welfare‑equivalent GDP**, since environmental damages become more severe at higher income levels. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7875433/)
- However, there is no paper that explicitly models “with strict Formosa‑style enforcement, Vietnam’s GDP in 2020 would be X% lower/higher”—the trade‑off is framed qualitatively and via CO₂ and pollution metrics.

## 11. How far can we push counterfactual GDP series?

Summing up the evidence:

- **Strong quantified counterfactuals:**
  - WTO accession with vs without SOEs: productivity gains from trade about **66% larger** in a counterfactual economy without SOEs, implying significantly higher manufacturing TFP and GDP. [cepr](https://cepr.org/voxeu/columns/globalisation-and-state-capitalism-assessing-effects-vietnams-wto-entry)
  - Infrastructure allocation: inland‑focused road investment yields **72% higher welfare gains** than actual allocation; this translates to roughly an extra 1–1.5 percentage points of GDP‑equivalent welfare relative to no‑investment baselines. [voxdev](https://voxdev.org/topic/energy-environment/harms-way-climate-change-and-road-investment-vietnam)

  - Macroeconomic stabilization: slower tightening would have prolonged high inflation and macro instability, lowering medium‑term GDP. [econweb.ucsd](https://econweb.ucsd.edu/~rstarr/191AB%20Fall%202016%20Winter%202017/ExemplaryPapers2016/Economic%20Reform%20in%20Vietnam.pdf)
  - Credit boom: stricter macroprudential policy would modestly reduce short‑term growth but likely raise medium‑term GDP by reducing inflation and financial‑stability risks. [seacen](https://www.seacen.org/publications/RStudies/2018/RP103/6-MacroprudentialPolicyVietnam-Chapter_5.pdf)
  - FDI regime: maintaining tight JV/local‑content constraints would likely lower FDI inflows and industrial exports, with lower GDP in the 1990s–2000s. [economica](https://www.economica.vn/Content/files/PUBL%20&%20REP/30%20Year%20of%20FDI%20in%20Vietnam.pdf)
  - Vinashin/SOEs: hard budget constraints would have limited over‑investment and reduced fiscal and banking‑sector stress, implying slightly higher medium‑term GDP. [studocu](https://www.studocu.vn/vn/document/truong-dai-hoc-van-lang/tai-chinh-quoc-te/case-study-1-vietnam-the-vinashin-scandal-2010-analysis-and-lessons-learned/149883610)
  - Education: earlier tertiary/vocational expansion would raise human capital and facilitate industrial upgrading, increasing long‑run GDP relative to the actual path. [jst.tnu.edu](https://jst.tnu.edu.vn/jst/article/download/1429/pdf)
  - Formosa/environment: stricter enforcement shifts FDI composition and lowers environmental costs; long‑run welfare‑equivalent GDP likely higher even if some dirty FDI is deterred. [studocu](https://www.studocu.vn/vn/document/royal-melbourne-institute-of-technology-vietnam/big-data-processing/formosa/90080326)
- **Conceptual but not numerically pinned down:**
  - Agricultural reform pace: literature shows large gains from the 1988 rapid de‑collectivization, implying that a more gradual Chinese path would have left early‑1990s GDP meaningfully lower, but does not quantify the gap. [digitalcommons.salve](https://digitalcommons.salve.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1020&context=fac_staff_pub)
  - US normalization speed: BTA and later agreements clearly boosted exports and growth; a “Myanmar‑style” cautious engagement would have reduced these gains, but quantitative GDP losses from preserving an embargo or partial normalization are not modeled. [insight.dickinsonlaw.psu](https://insight.dickinsonlaw.psu.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1354&context=psilr)

If you want to operationalize this in a research exercise, the most defensible quantitative anchors are:

- Use Baccini et al.’s WTO‑SOE simulations for trade‑related productivity counterfactuals. [papers.ssrn](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3036319)
- Use the Yale/VoxDev road‑investment welfare multipliers for infrastructure spatial allocation. [voxdev](https://voxdev.org/topic/energy-environment/harms-way-climate-change-and-road-investment-vietnam)
- Use IMF credit‑growth and inflation data plus their qualitative assessments to bound costs of the credit boom, and Vinashin asset/debt magnitudes to approximate a 1–3% of GDP range for crisis‑related fiscal/financial losses. [imf](https://www.imf.org/external/pubs/ft/scr/2010/cr10281.pdf)

Everything else will need to be framed as scenario analysis calibrated with these partial estimates rather than full‑fledged, literature‑supplied counterfactual GDP trajectories.
