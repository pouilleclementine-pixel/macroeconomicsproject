I. Institutions as the Fundamental Cause

The Doi Moi Story

Consider a natural experiment: same country, same culture, same people. In 1985, Vietnam's inflation exceeded 700%, GDP per capita sat at roughly $200, and Soviet aid of $4 million per day kept the economy afloat. Then came institutional reform. Thirty-five years later: exports 200-fold higher, poverty from 58% to under 4%, GDP per capita at $5,026. The variable that changed? Institutions.

This aligns with the Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson institutional framework (2024 Nobel laureates). Just as North Korea and South Korea diverged through institutions alone, Vietnam's Đổi Mới ("renovation") in 1986 marks the institutional inflection point that converted extractive structures into inclusive ones. Property rights emerged. Markets replaced central planning. The result was not gradual improvement but exponential transformation.

1986
Doi Moi launches at 6th Party Congress. Market mechanisms enter planned economy. Inflation brought from 700%+ to single digits.
1995
ASEAN membership. Integration into regional trade networks begins.
2001
US-Vietnam Bilateral Trade Agreement. Vietnam granted Normal Trade Relations status. Bilateral trade surged from $1.5B (2001) to $90B+ (2021).
2007
WTO accession after 12 years of negotiations. Full entry into global trading system.
2018-19
CPTPP and EVFTA ratified. 99% tariff elimination with EU. Access to 53 countries covering 87% of world GDP.
2025
Doi Moi 2.0 announced. Consolidate 63 to 34 provinces, reduce bureaucracy 30%. Institutional reform continues.

Vietnam's average tariff fell from over 23% in the mid-1990s to approximately 1.1% today. This institutional opening created the conditions for the FDI surge that followed.

Samsung alone accounts for 13.12% of Vietnam's GDP.

A single Korean conglomerate generates more than Vietnam's entire agricultural sector—and half of all its exports.

II. Capital Deepening and the Solow Prediction

The FDI Engine

Vietnam's growth story is textbook capital deepening. Massive foreign capital inflows drove rapid GDP growth. But the Solow model predicts diminishing returns without productivity gains. Vietnam's ICOR—the Incremental Capital-Output Ratio, measuring how many dollars of investment it takes to generate one dollar of GDP growth—typically runs 5.5-6.0, spiking during COVID-19 to 14-15 before returning to ~5.1 in 2022. The pattern signals capital-intensive growth requiring sustained efficiency gains.

The Soviet Parallel

The USSR grew rapidly through capital accumulation until return on capital fell from 30% (1950) to 0% (1990). Without TFP growth, the trajectory plateaued. Vietnam's ICOR trend echoes this pattern. Each additional unit of GDP now requires more investment than before.

GDP Growth = TFP + α×Labor + (1-α)×Capital
Vietnam's growth is overwhelmingly capital (K). R&D spending at 0.43% of GDP signals minimal TFP contribution. This is the vulnerability.

III. The Expenditure Identity Paradox

What Vietnam Actually Exports

Vietnam's NX looks spectacular at $475B exports. But in the expenditure identity (Y = C + I + G + NX), imports are subtracted because consumption, investment, and government spending include both domestic and imported goods. Vietnam's foreign value-added is 48%, meaning nearly half the value of each export dollar was imported as components.

The Screwdriver Economy

Here is the counterintuitive finding: foreign value-added in Vietnamese exports ROSE from 36% (2005) to 48% (2020). Despite exports growing spectacularly, Vietnam captures LESS of each dollar proportionally. The country assembles; it does not create.

IV. The Demographic Dividend and the Closing Window

Vietnam's Race Against Time

The three-stage demographic dividend framework illuminates Vietnam's position. Vietnam sits squarely in Stage 2—the dividend window—when fertility has fallen but the population hasn't yet aged. Fertility is at 1.94 (below replacement of 2.1). Working-age share is 67.7%. The window closes within the next decade as Vietnam enters an aging society phase.

The agricultural reallocation channel offers insight into TFP growth. Employment shifted from ~50% (2008) to 26.9% (2024) in agriculture. Urbanization accelerated from 25% (1999) to ~40% today. This structural transformation—workers moving from lower-productivity agriculture to higher-productivity manufacturing—is a classic driver of productivity gains. But this channel is running out of room. The remaining agricultural workers are harder to relocate, and the easy gains are behind us.

The Skills Constraint

Vietnam has approximately 1,400 chip engineers and 5,000 chip design graduates total. Strategy 1018 targets 50,000 semiconductor engineers by 2030. Without this human capital upgrade, the country cannot move up the value chain before the demographic window closes.

Only 34 of ~142 middle-income countries have ever made it to high-income status.

The odds are roughly 1 in 4. 108 remain stuck today.

V. Trade Policy Analysis

Tariffs, Risks, and the Road Ahead

Before advocating for tariffs, rigorous analysis requires asking three critical questions. Applying this framework to the US-Vietnam trade relationship reveals complex dynamics:

Three Critical Questions for Tariff Analysis
A framework for evaluating trade policy | Source: Vietnam Briefing, VoxChina
Question Application to Vietnam
"What is the actual problem?" US runs a $123.5B trade deficit with Vietnam. Concerns about transshipment of Chinese goods: ~16.5% of exports to US involve rerouting; 1.8M tons mislabeled aluminum discovered 2019.
"Is reducing imports the right solution?" Vietnam's exports to US contain ~48% imported inputs (much from China). Tariffs on Vietnam may not solve the China problem; they just redirect the assembly location again.
"Are tariffs the best tool?" 46% reciprocal tariff (negotiated to 20%) risks disrupting supply chains for US companies (Apple, Nike, Intel) that chose Vietnam precisely to diversify from China. The tariff hits US firms' own supply chains.

Aggregate Supply Analysis

For Vietnam, the 46% tariff functions as a negative aggregate supply shock. It raises the cost of accessing its largest export market. This creates stagflationary pressure: output falls, costs rise. The AS curve shifts left, reducing equilibrium output while putting upward pressure on prices.

The State Bank of Vietnam is modernizing monetary policy. SBV removes credit quotas starting 2026, moving from quantity-based money supply control to a price-based system. This shift reflects modern central banking practice: controlling money creation through interest rates rather than direct quantity targets. The 16% credit growth cap for 2025 represents the old regime; 2026 marks the transition with a target of 15%.

The Upgrading Strategy

Strategy 1018 (formula: C = SET + 1, for Chips, Specialization, Electronics, Talent + Vietnam) targets semiconductor leadership. VinFast represents the domestic champion attempt: ~197,000 EVs delivered in 2025 (+102% YoY), ~26.7% domestic market share, ~60% localization rate with a target of 80%+ by 2026.

The Core Tension

Vietnam has 10-12 years before its demographic dividend ends. GDP growth hit 8.02% in 2025, the strongest since 2011. But the ICOR is rising, R&D is minimal, and 71-74% of exports come from foreign firms. The Asian Tigers at similar income levels already had Samsung and Hyundai. Vietnam must build domestic capabilities while the window remains open.
